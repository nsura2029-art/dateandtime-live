Time Zones Guide

What is a Time Zone?

A complete guide to how the world measures time — UTC offsets, IANA zones, the prime meridian, and why some countries are offset by 30 or 45 minutes.

Updated July 20, 2026 · 8 min read

The short answer

A time zone is a region where the same standard time is used. The world is divided into 40 named offset zones — from UTC-12 to UTC+14 — plus hundreds of regional variants (called IANA time zones) that track each region's specific daylight saving time (DST) rules.

Why time zones exist

Earth rotates 360° in 24 hours, so the sun appears to move 15° of longitude per hour across the sky. If every place kept its own local solar time (e.g. "sunrise is always 6 AM"), clocks would differ by about 4 minutes for every degree of longitude — by the time you traveled 1,000 km east or west, your watch would be off by nearly an hour from your neighbor's.

Standardized time zones solve this by having nearby places agree on a single clock time. Most time zones are aligned to whole hours from UTC (Coordinated Universal Time), though several countries use 30 or 45-minute offsets for historical or political reasons.

The prime meridian

In 1884, the International Meridian Conference established a single reference point for timekeeping: the prime meridian, defined as 0° longitude, passing through the Royal Observatory in Greenwich, London. Every other time zone is defined by its distance east (+) or west (−) of the prime meridian, measured in hours and minutes.

For example, New York is 5 hours west of Greenwich, so it uses UTC-5 in winter (Eastern Standard Time). Tokyo is 9 hours east of Greenwich, so it uses UTC+9 year-round (Japan Standard Time).

UTC, GMT, and the world's time standard

UTC (Coordinated Universal Time) is the world's primary time standard, maintained by a network of over 400 atomic clocks coordinated by the International Bureau of Weights and Measures in France. UTC replaced GMT (Greenwich Mean Time) as the official reference in 1960, though the two terms are still often used interchangeably in casual contexts.

The key difference: GMT is based on the Earth's rotation (solar time at Greenwich), while UTC is based on atomic clocks. For practical purposes they're within 1 second of each other, but UTC is more stable and is used by all modern computing, aviation, and scientific systems.

Half-hour and 45-minute offsets

Several countries use time zones that are not offset by whole hours. These exist for historical, geographical, or political reasons:

  • India (UTC+5:30) — India adopted a single time zone for the entire country in 1906, offset by 5 hours 30 minutes from UTC. The 30 minutes puts India's noon roughly at the geographic center of the country.
  • Iran (UTC+3:30) — Iran uses a 30-minute offset, partially to differentiate its time from neighboring countries.
  • Afghanistan (UTC+4:30) — Adopted a 30-minute offset in 1947.
  • Myanmar (UTC+6:30) — Myanmar diverged from Indian Standard Time in 1962 to assert national identity.
  • Parts of Australia (UTC+9:30 and UTC+10:30) — South Australia and the Northern Territory use 30-minute offsets, as does Lord Howe Island (UTC+10:30 with DST to UTC+11:00).
  • Nepal (UTC+5:45) — The only country with a 45-minute offset. Adopted in 1986 to distinguish itself from India.
  • Chatham Islands, New Zealand (UTC+12:45) — Uses 45-minute DST offsets, the only place in the world to do so.

IANA time zones: the global standard

The IANA (Internet Assigned Numbers Authority) Time Zone Database — also called tzdata or zoneinfo — is the de facto global standard for time zone identifiers. Every computer, phone, and server that needs to know "what time is it in Tokyo?" looks up Asia/Tokyo in this database.

IANA zones follow a Continent/City (or Continent/Region/Subregion) naming convention. Some examples:

  • America/New_York — Eastern Time (US & Canada)
  • America/Los_Angeles — Pacific Time (US & Canada)
  • Europe/London — UK (GMT / BST)
  • Europe/Paris — Central European Time (France, Germany, etc.)
  • Asia/Kolkata — India (UTC+5:30, no DST)
  • Asia/Tokyo — Japan (UTC+9, no DST)
  • Australia/Sydney — Australian Eastern Time (with DST)
  • Pacific/Auckland — New Zealand (with DST)

There are currently around 408 IANA time zones, including aliases (e.g. US/Pacific is an alias for America/Los_Angeles). The database is maintained by a group of volunteers and is updated several times per year as countries change their DST rules.

Daylight Saving Time (DST)

About 70 countries observe Daylight Saving Time (DST), shifting clocks forward by 1 hour during warmer months so evenings have more daylight. The rules vary by country:

  • United States & Canada: Spring forward on the 2nd Sunday in March, fall back on the 1st Sunday in November.
  • European Union: Last Sunday in March to last Sunday in October (though the EU ended clock changes in 2026).
  • Australia & New Zealand: Southern hemisphere DST runs opposite to the northern hemisphere (October to April).
  • Most of Africa and Asia: Do not observe DST. Countries like Japan, China, India, and Singapore stay on a single offset year-round.

How time zones affect daily life

Time zones matter for:

  • International business — scheduling meetings across continents without waking someone at 3 AM
  • Travel — adjusting to jet lag as you cross time zones
  • Financial markets — NYSE opens at 9:30 AM ET, LSE opens at 8:00 AM GMT, TSE opens at 9:00 AM JST
  • Airlines and aviation — all flight times are quoted in local time plus a time zone offset
  • Computing — servers log events in UTC, applications convert to local time for display
  • Satellite communications and GPS — all use UTC internally to avoid time zone confusion

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a time zone?

A time zone is a geographic region where the same standard time is used. The world is divided into 40 named offset zones (from UTC-12 to UTC+14) plus hundreds of regional IANA zones (like America/New_York or Asia/Kolkata) that track each region's specific DST rules.

How many time zones are there in the world?

There are 40 named offset time zones (UTC-12 to UTC+14) and approximately 408 IANA time zones when you account for regional variations. Each IANA zone tracks the full history of DST rules for that specific region.

Why are there 24 time zones and not more?

Earth rotates 360° in 24 hours, so the sun moves 15° per hour. In theory, this gives 24 hour-wide time zones. In practice, most countries have adopted whole-hour offsets from UTC, but some use 30-minute (India UTC+5:30) or 45-minute (Nepal UTC+5:45) offsets for historical or political reasons.

What is the prime meridian?

The prime meridian is the line of 0° longitude, defined by international agreement in 1884 to pass through the Royal Observatory in Greenwich, London. All time zones are defined by their offset east (+) or west (−) of the prime meridian.

What is an IANA time zone?

IANA (Internet Assigned Numbers Authority) time zones are the global standard for time zone identifiers. They use the format Continent/City (like America/New_York, Europe/London, Asia/Tokyo) and are stored in the IANA Time Zone Database (also called tzdata or zoneinfo), which is updated multiple times per year to reflect changes in DST rules worldwide.
