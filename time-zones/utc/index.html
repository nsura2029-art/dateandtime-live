Time Zones Guide

UTC & GMT: The World's Time Standard

What is UTC? What's the difference between UTC and GMT? How do atomic clocks keep the world's time? Everything you need to know.

Updated July 20, 2026 · 7 min read

What is UTC?

UTC (Coordinated Universal Time) is the world's primary time standard. It is the reference point from which all other time zones are calculated. Every time zone in the world is defined by its offset east (+) or west (−) of UTC.

For example:

  • New York is UTC-5 in winter (Eastern Standard Time) and UTC-4 in summer (Eastern Daylight Time)
  • Tokyo is UTC+9 year-round (Japan Standard Time)
  • India is UTC+5:30 year-round (India Standard Time, no DST)
  • Sydney is UTC+10 in winter and UTC+11 in summer (AEDT)

UTC is the same everywhere on Earth — it doesn't change with seasons, doesn't observe DST, and isn't tied to any specific location.

UTC vs GMT: what's the difference?

The terms UTC and GMT are often used interchangeably, but they're technically different:

  • GMT (Greenwich Mean Time) is a time zone. It was originally defined as mean solar time at the Royal Observatory in Greenwich, London. It varies slightly with the Earth's rotation (which is affected by tides, geological activity, and other factors).
  • UTC (Coordinated Universal Time) is a time standard. It's based on a weighted average of over 400 atomic clocks worldwide and is far more stable than solar time.

For everyday purposes, UTC and GMT are the same to within a second. The difference is mostly of interest to scientists and engineers who need extreme precision. However, when you see "GMT" in casual contexts (like a UK newspaper saying "GMT 14:30"), it really means UTC — the UK uses GMT as its standard time, but the underlying time reference is now UTC.

How UTC is maintained

UTC is maintained by the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM) in Sèvres, France. The BIPM is the international authority for measurement standards, established by the Metre Convention in 1875.

The BIPM coordinates data from over 400 atomic clocks at national metrology institutes in 50+ countries, including:

  • NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) — United States
  • NPL (National Physical Laboratory) — United Kingdom
  • PTB (Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt) — Germany
  • NIST (National Institute of Information and Communications Technology) — Japan
  • NMIA (National Measurement Institute) — Australia

These clocks are based on cesium-133 atoms, which vibrate at exactly 9,192,631,770 times per second. This is the official definition of the second, established in 1967. The BIPM uses this "atomic second" to compute a stable, accurate international time scale, which becomes UTC.

Why UTC replaced GMT

GMT was the world's time standard from 1884 until 1960, when it was replaced by UTC for several reasons:

  1. GMT was variable. Solar time at Greenwich drifts slightly as the Earth's rotation changes (the day gets longer by ~2 milliseconds per century due to tidal friction).
  2. Atomic clocks were more accurate. By 1960, cesium atomic clocks were 100,000x more stable than astronomical observations.
  3. Scientific needs demanded precision. Space exploration, radio astronomy, and particle physics all needed time measurements far more precise than solar-based GMT.

UTC was officially adopted on January 1, 1960 by international agreement, with atomic clocks maintained by the US Naval Observatory and the UK National Physical Laboratory.

Leap seconds

Because atomic time (UTC) drifts from solar time (UT1) as the Earth's rotation slows, the BIPM occasionally adds a leap second to keep them aligned. Leap seconds are added at the end of June 30 or December 31, and the BIPM announces them about 6 months in advance.

Since 1972, 27 leap seconds have been added — most recently on December 31, 2016. The Earth's rotation is currently slowing, so leap seconds are always positive (we add a second, never subtract).

Leap seconds have caused several high-profile IT outages, including:

  • 2012: LinkedIn, Reddit, and FourSquare experienced downtime
  • 2015: A leap second inserted in mid-2015 caused outages at Twitter, Instagram, Pinterest, Netflix, and Airbnb
  • 2016: Cloudflare, Amazon, and others reported issues

Many tech companies (Google, Amazon, Facebook) have implemented "leap smearing" — distributing the extra second across 24 hours — to avoid sudden jumps. There is ongoing international debate about whether to abolish leap seconds entirely by 2035.

UTC in everyday life

You might not realize it, but UTC is everywhere in modern technology:

  • Aviation: All flight plans, air traffic control, and airline operations use UTC to avoid confusion between time zones. Pilots call it "Zulu time."
  • Computing: Servers store all timestamps in UTC internally. The Unix epoch (January 1, 1970, 00:00:00 UTC) is the reference point for all Unix timestamps.
  • GPS and satellite: GPS satellites carry atomic clocks synchronized to UTC. Your phone's GPS lock uses UTC for all position calculations.
  • Internet standards: HTTP, SSL/TLS, email (SMTP), and most network protocols use UTC timestamps in their headers.
  • Databases: Most modern databases (PostgreSQL, MySQL, etc.) recommend storing all timestamps in UTC and converting to local time only for display.
  • Programming: Every modern programming language has built-in support for UTC timestamps (JavaScript's Date.now(), Python's datetime.utcnow(), etc.).

How to read UTC times

UTC times are often written with a "Z" suffix (for "Zulu", the NATO phonetic alphabet letter that means UTC) or with an explicit "+00:00" offset. For example:

  • 14:30:00Z = 2:30 PM UTC = 10:30 AM EDT (New York) = 11:30 PM JST (Tokyo)
  • 2026-07-20T15:00:00+00:00 = ISO 8601 format for July 20, 2026 at 3:00 PM UTC
  • 14:30 UTC = 14:30 in coordinated universal time

The Z suffix is widely used in aviation, military, and computing to avoid confusion with local time zones.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is UTC?

UTC (Coordinated Universal Time) is the world's primary time standard, maintained by a network of over 400 atomic clocks coordinated by the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM). It replaced GMT in 1960.

What is the difference between UTC and GMT?

GMT is a time zone based on solar time at Greenwich. UTC is a time standard based on atomic clocks. For most practical purposes, they're the same to within a second, but UTC is more stable and is the official reference for modern computing, aviation, and science.

Who maintains UTC?

UTC is maintained by the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM) in Sèvres, France. The BIPM combines data from over 400 atomic clocks at national laboratories in 50+ countries.

Is UTC affected by Daylight Saving Time?

No. UTC never changes — it has no DST. All other time zones are defined by their offset from UTC. When a country springs forward for DST, its offset from UTC changes by 1 hour, but UTC itself stays the same.

How accurate is UTC?

UTC is accurate to within 1 second of mean solar time, but the underlying atomic clock readings are accurate to within nanoseconds per day. The official UTC timescale is computed monthly by the BIPM.
