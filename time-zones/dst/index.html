Daylight Saving Time (DST) 2026

Everything you need to know about DST in 2026: when to spring forward and fall back, which countries observe it, and why it exists.

Updated July 20, 2026 · 6 min read

What is Daylight Saving Time?

Daylight Saving Time (DST) is the practice of advancing clocks by 1 hour during warmer months so that evenings have more daylight. In spring, clocks "spring forward" (lose an hour of sleep); in autumn, they "fall back" (gain an hour). The idea is to shift an hour of daylight from the morning to the evening, when most people are awake and active.

For example, when DST is active in New York, the time zone changes from UTC-5 (Eastern Standard Time) to UTC-4 (Eastern Daylight Time). At 7:00 AM EST, it's still 7:00 AM but the sun rose at 6:00 AM instead of 7:00 AM, and will set at 8:00 PM instead of 7:00 PM.

Why do we have DST?

Benjamin Franklin first proposed the concept of DST in 1784 in a satirical letter to the Journal of Paris, suggesting that Parisians could save money on candles by waking earlier in summer. The idea was first seriously implemented by Germany in 1916 during World War I to conserve coal and energy.

The UK, the US, and many other countries adopted DST soon after. The original rationale — saving energy by reducing evening artificial lighting — has been debated for over a century. Modern studies have found mixed results: some show modest energy savings, others show little to no net effect, and a 2008 study for Indiana (which adopted DST in 2006) found a 1% increase in energy use due to higher air-conditioning demand.

Other commonly cited benefits include:

  • More daylight for outdoor recreation and exercise after work
  • Reduced traffic accidents (more daylight during evening rush hour)
  • Boost to retail and tourism industries
  • Alignment of school hours with daylight for child safety

Which countries observe DST?

About 70 countries worldwide observe DST, but the majority of the world's population does not live in DST-observing countries. The pattern roughly breaks down as follows:

  • Observes DST: Most of North America (US, Canada, Mexico, except Sonora and Quintana Roo), most of Europe, parts of South America (Chile, Paraguay, Uruguay), Australia (only some states), New Zealand, parts of the Middle East (Iran, Israel, Palestine, Lebanon, Syria, Jordan), Cuba, Haiti, Bermuda, Greenland, and a handful of others.
  • Does NOT observe DST: Most of Africa (all 54 countries, with rare exceptions like Namibia and Egypt historically), most of Asia (China, Japan, India, Singapore, South Korea, Hong Kong, Taiwan, Indonesia, Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam, and all of South & Central Asia), most of South America (Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Colombia, Peru, Venezuela), Hawaii, Arizona (US, except Navajo Nation), and most equatorial regions.

Notably, China used to observe DST between 1986 and 1991, but abandoned it in 1992. Russia observed year-round DST from 2011 to 2014, then switched to year-round standard time in 2014 (effectively ending DST). Turkey permanently adopted DST (UTC+3) in 2016.

The EU's 2026 DST change

In March 2019, the European Parliament voted to end the biannual clock change across the EU by 2021. Each member state was supposed to choose whether to stay on permanent summer time (DST) or permanent winter time (standard time), with the change taking effect in 2021.

However, the directive was never fully implemented due to a combination of:

  • The COVID-19 pandemic disrupting legislative schedules
  • Lack of coordination between member states (which would have created economic chaos if some countries chose permanent summer time and others permanent winter time)
  • Public consultation showing divided opinion

As of 2026, the EU has formally abolished the requirement to observe DST, but most member states continue to do so in practice. EU residents will still see their clocks change in March and October 2026.

Health and productivity effects of DST

Modern research has highlighted several downsides of DST clock changes:

  • Sleep disruption — The "spring forward" transition is associated with a spike in heart attacks, strokes, and traffic accidents in the days following the change.
  • Productivity loss — Studies show workplace injuries increase by ~5.7% and "cyberloafing" (non-work internet use) increases in the days after the spring transition.
  • Mood effects — Some research links DST to increased depression and reduced well-being, particularly in northern latitudes with already-short winter days.
  • Circadian disruption — Even small 1-hour shifts in light exposure can take days to weeks for the body to adjust.

The American Medical Association, American Academy of Sleep Medicine, and many other medical bodies have called for an end to biannual clock changes.

Tips for adjusting to DST

  • Start adjusting a few days early — shift your bedtime by 15 minutes each day for the 4 days before the change.
  • Get morning sunlight — bright light exposure in the morning helps reset your circadian rhythm faster.
  • Avoid caffeine and alcohol in the hours before bed on the transition days.
  • Be careful driving on the Monday after the spring change — accident rates spike ~6%.
  • Update all your devices — most phones and computers update automatically, but analog clocks, microwaves, and car clocks need manual adjustment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Daylight Saving Time (DST)?

Daylight Saving Time (DST) is the practice of advancing clocks by 1 hour during warmer months so that evenings have more daylight. About 70 countries observe DST.

When does DST start in 2026 (US)?

In the United States, DST 2026 begins on Sunday, March 8, 2026 at 2:00 AM local time. Clocks spring forward 1 hour to 3:00 AM. DST ends on Sunday, November 1, 2026 at 2:00 AM, when clocks fall back to 1:00 AM.

Which countries observe DST?

About 70 countries observe DST, including the US, Canada, Mexico, EU, UK, Australia, New Zealand, Chile, and Paraguay. Most of Africa, Asia, and the equatorial regions do not observe DST.

Why do we have DST?

DST was first proposed by Benjamin Franklin in 1784 and first widely adopted during World War I to save energy. Modern studies are mixed on whether DST actually saves energy.

Does the European Union still change clocks in 2026?

The EU voted to end clock changes in 2019, and the directive was formally abolished in 2026, but most member states continue to observe DST in practice during 2026.
