# Vietnam Adds New Public Holiday

Vietnam is set to add a new **paid public holiday on November 24**, taking effect in 2026. The new holiday will recognize and promote Vietnamese cultural heritage as the country continues its rapid economic growth.

## The New Holiday

The new holiday, signed into law in late 2025, commemorates Vietnamese culture and heritage. The exact name and theme of the holiday are still being finalized, but the date — **November 24** — was chosen to coincide with the existing celebration of Vietnamese Teachers' Day, expanding what is typically a one-day observance into a longer cultural celebration.

This brings Vietnam's total paid public holidays to **12 days per year**, up from 11.

## Vietnam's Public Holiday Calendar (2026)

| Date | Holiday |
|---|---|
| Jan 1 | New Year's Day (Tết Dương lịch) |
| Jan 28 – Feb 3 | Tết Nguyên Đán (Lunar New Year) |
| Apr 26 | Reunification Day |
| May 1 | Labour Day |
| Sep 2 | National Day |
| Sep 24 – 26 | Mid-Autumn Festival (Tết Trung Thu)* |
| **Nov 24** | **Vietnamese Culture Day (new)** |
| Plus various Buddhist and ancestor veneration days | |

*Some are observed informally by businesses but not all are paid.*

## Why Now?

Vietnam's economy has been among the fastest-growing in Southeast Asia, with GDP growth averaging **6–7% annually** over the past decade. The government has been using cultural holidays to:

1. **Promote domestic tourism** — particularly during shoulder seasons
2. **Strengthen national identity** — especially among younger, urban Vietnamese
3. **Boost the creative economy** — including traditional crafts, music, and food

## Impact on Workers and Businesses

For the estimated **33 million formal-sector workers** in Vietnam, the new holiday means:

- One additional paid day off
- Higher consumer spending around cultural events
- Slight disruption to international business calls (similar to the existing lunar new year break)

For foreign companies operating in Vietnam, this means planning around another holiday. The November date falls at a relatively quiet time on the global business calendar, so disruption should be minimal.
