# Friday the 13th: The Superstition Explained

If you have **paraskevidekatriaphobia** (the fear of Friday the 13th), you're not alone — an estimated **21 million Americans** are affected, costing the US economy an estimated **$800 million to $900 million** in lost productivity every time the 13th falls on a Friday.

## Why Is It Unlucky?

The superstition has **three historical threads** that combined over centuries:

### 1. Norse Mythology

In Norse mythology, **Loki**, the trickster god, was the 13th to arrive at a feast of the gods in Valhalla. He tricked Höðr into killing the god Baldr, plunging the world into darkness. The number 13 became associated with mischief, betrayal, and death.

### 2. Christianity

- At the **Last Supper**, there were 13 people present (Jesus + 12 apostles). The 13th to arrive was **Judas Iscariot**, the betrayer
- Friday was the day of the crucifixion, and was already associated with bad luck
- **Cain killed Abel on a Friday the 13th** (in some traditions)

### 3. The Knights Templar

On **Friday, October 13, 1307**, King Philip IV of France ordered the mass arrest of the Knights Templar. Many were tortured and executed. This event is often cited as the origin of the modern superstition, though historians debate whether it actually caught on until centuries later.

### 4. The Novel

**Thomas W. Lawson's 1907 novel "Friday, the Thirteenth"** popularized the modern superstition. The story is about a stockbroker who uses the date to cause a stock market crash. The book's success cemented the date in popular culture.

## Around the World

The unlucky number and day vary by culture:

- **Greece, Spain, Latin America**: Tuesday the 13th (martes 13) is unlucky — *martes* comes from Mars, the god of war
- **Italy**: Friday the 17th is unlucky — the number 13 is considered lucky in Italy
- **China, Japan, Korea**: The number **4** is unlucky (sounds like "death" in Chinese)
- **India**: The number **0** is considered unlucky
