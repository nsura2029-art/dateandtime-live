Perseid Meteor Shower 2026: When and How to Watch

📅 Published July 20, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read

Perseid Meteor Shower 2026

The Perseid meteor shower is back in 2026, peaking on the night of August 12-13. With up to 100 meteors per hour at peak, it's one of the most reliable and prolific meteor showers of the year — and a favorite for casual stargazers.

Best viewing time: After midnight local time, when the radiant is highest in the sky. The pre-dawn hours of August 13 are typically optimal.

Moon phase for 2026: A waning crescent moon (about 18% illuminated) will be rising late in the night, so moonlight will be minimal — excellent viewing conditions.

Where to Look

The Perseids appear to radiate from the constellation Perseus, which rises in the northeast after sunset. You don't need to look directly at Perseus, though — meteors closer to the radiant will have shorter trails, while those farther away will have longer, more dramatic streaks across the sky.

A good rule: lie flat on your back, look straight up, and let your eyes take in as much sky as possible.

Viewing Tips

  1. Find dark skies: Get away from city lights. Even suburbs can cut your meteor count by 50%+
  2. No equipment needed: Telescopes and binoculars actually limit what you see — use just your eyes
  3. Allow 20–30 minutes for your eyes to dark-adapt after looking at your phone
  4. Bring a reclining chair or blanket: You'll be looking up for hours
  5. Dress warmly: Even in August, night temperatures can drop significantly

Where the Perseids Come From

The Perseids are caused by debris left behind by Comet 109P/Swift-Tuttle, which orbits the sun every 133 years. As Earth crosses the comet's orbital path each August, tiny particles (most no larger than a grain of sand) burn up in our atmosphere at 59 km/s, creating the bright streaks we call "shooting stars."

Comet Swift-Tuttle last passed through the inner solar system in 1992 and won't return until 2126 — but the debris trail it left behind keeps the show going for centuries.
